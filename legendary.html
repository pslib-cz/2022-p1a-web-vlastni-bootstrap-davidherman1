the Eastern Cape dwarf bullfrog

Breviceps fuscus, commonly known as the Eastern Cape dwarf bullfrog, is a species of frog found in the Eastern Cape region of South Africa. They are relatively small in size, with males growing to a maximum length of about 3 centimeters, while females are slightly larger. These frogs are known for their distinctive, low-pitched calls that sound like a deep "wark." They are primarily terrestrial and can be found in a variety of habitats, including grasslands, forests, and savannas. Breviceps fuscus is currently considered a species of least concern, although like many amphibians, they could face threats from habitat loss and climate change in the future.

the Glass frog

Glass frogs are a group of small, semi-transparent frogs found in the rainforests of Central and South America. They are named for their translucent skin, which allows their internal organs to be visible through their abdomen. This unique adaptation helps them blend into their surroundings and avoid predators. Glass frogs are also known for their distinctive, loud calls, which can be heard during the breeding season. These frogs are primarily arboreal, spending much of their time in trees and other vegetation near bodies of water. While some species of glass frogs are abundant, others are threatened by habitat loss, pollution, and the pet trade. Conservation efforts, such as protecting critical habitats and regulating the trade of wild-caught frogs, are important for their survival.

the Warty newt

the Warty newt, is a species of aquatic salamander found in Europe, particularly in eastern and central regions. They are named for their distinctive warty skin, which helps them blend into their surroundings and avoid predators. Warty newts can range in color from dark brown to olive-green and have a flattened head with a pointed snout. These salamanders are primarily aquatic, living in ponds, streams, and other bodies of water, where they feed on small invertebrates. During the breeding season, males develop a conspicuous crest along their back and tail. While Warty newts are currently considered a species of least concern, they face threats from habitat loss and fragmentation, pollution, and the introduction of non-native species. Conservation efforts, such as preserving and restoring wetland habitats, are essential for their survival.
